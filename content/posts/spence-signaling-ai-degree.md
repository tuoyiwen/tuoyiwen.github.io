**Spence's 1973 signaling theory explains why your degree is losing value — and what's replacing it.**

Lately, my PhD friends and I keep circling the same question: when AI can write your papers, run your data, and review your literature — what exactly is your degree still proving?

No one had a good answer.

This morning, scrolling Twitter, I stumbled on a post about economist Michael Spence's 1973 paper *Job Market Signaling*. And suddenly the question had a framework.

Spence argued, 53 years ago, that your degree never proved what you *learned*. It proved what you could *endure*. He won a Nobel Prize for this insight.

But here's the problem: AI just made the endurance part easy. So does the signal still hold?

---

## The Idea That Changed Labor Economics

Before Spence, the consensus was straightforward. Gary Becker's **human capital theory** (1964) said: education makes you more productive. You learn skills, you acquire knowledge, you become a better worker. Go to school → learn things → earn more. Simple.

Spence asked a subversive question: *what if education doesn't teach you anything useful at all — and it's still worth getting?*

His answer rested on three assumptions:

**Assumption 1: Information asymmetry.** Employers can't directly observe how productive you are before they hire you.

**Assumption 2: Differential cost.** If you're talented, completing a degree is relatively easy — less time, less effort, less suffering. If you're less talented, it's harder. The cost of education is negatively correlated with ability.

**Assumption 3: Separating equilibrium.** Because of that cost difference, talented people choose to get degrees and less talented people don't. Employers observe the degree and rationally infer higher ability. The signal works.

This is **signaling theory**. Spence won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2001.

Bryan Caplan, in his 2019 book *The Case Against Education*, pushed this further. He estimated that roughly **80% of the wage premium** from education comes from signaling, not from actual skill building. His key evidence? The "sheepskin effect" — students who complete 3.5 years of college but drop out earn dramatically less than those who finish. The knowledge is nearly identical. The signal is not.

---

## Now Add AI

Spence's theory stands on those three assumptions. AI is challenging every one of them.

### Assumption 1: Employers can't see your ability directly

This is less true than it used to be. GitHub profiles, Kaggle rankings, portfolio websites, AI-powered skill assessments — all of these make ability more directly observable. The information asymmetry is shrinking.

But it hasn't disappeared. Soft skills, judgment, long-term potential — these remain invisible on a resume.

### Assumption 2: Education is harder for less talented people

This is where AI hits hardest.

Spence's entire mechanism depends on a simple condition: the cost of education must be negatively correlated with ability. Smart people find school easy; less smart people find it hard. That's what makes the signal informative.

Now consider what AI tools do to academic work:

| Task | Before AI | After AI |
|------|-----------|----------|
| Writing an essay | Talent matters enormously | ChatGPT levels the field |
| Completing a coding assignment | Speed correlates with skill | Copilot makes everyone fast |
| Conducting research | Efficiency varies with ability | AI search and synthesis tools flatten the curve |

When a C-student can use Claude to produce A-grade essays, the cost differential collapses. The signal becomes noise.

### Assumption 3: The separating equilibrium holds

If Assumption 2 breaks, Assumption 3 follows. When education is cheap for everyone, everyone gets the degree. Economists call this **pooling equilibrium** — all types make the same choice, and the signal tells employers nothing.

There's a more common name for this: **degree inflation**. It's been building for decades. AI just hit the accelerator.

---

## The Signal Doesn't Die — It Migrates

So the diploma signal is breaking. Does that mean signaling is over?

No. And this is what I find most elegant about Spence's framework: when a signal loses its separating power, the market doesn't give up on signals. It searches for new ones. **The signaling game doesn't end — it migrates.**

If we follow Spence's logic, a new signal needs to satisfy the same conditions: it must be observable by employers, and it must be *harder to produce* for less capable people. So what qualifies?

**Demonstrated output.** Portfolios, public work, verifiable track records. This reduces information asymmetry directly — the employer can *see* your ability, not just infer it from a credential.

**AI-leverage ability.** Can you use AI tools to produce extraordinary results? This is itself a signal with a cost differential: talented people extract more value from AI, just as talented people extracted more value from education. The instrument changes; the Spence logic holds.

**Meta-cognitive skill.** Can you evaluate AI output, catch its errors, direct its reasoning? This is the new "hard thing" — and it still correlates with ability, which means it satisfies the Spence condition.

**Human-only contributions.** Judgment, ethical reasoning, leadership, relationship-building. AI provides no shortcut here, so these retain their separating power naturally.

## What This Means

I'm currently pursuing a PhD in Organizational Behaviour, and I have to be honest about three things — these are problems I'm facing right now:

**1. My degree is a depreciating asset.** Not worthless — but its signal strength weakens every year as AI makes academic achievement easier to produce. The half-life of a credential is shrinking.

**2. My portfolio is an appreciating asset.** Every published article, every verifiable project, every case study — these are signals harder to fake than a diploma. Build in public. Accumulate continuously.

**3. The real signal is how you use AI.** The new separating equilibrium isn't "people who resist AI vs. people who use AI." It's "people who use AI to mass-produce mediocre work vs. people who use AI to produce extraordinary work that reveals their judgment." The second group is small. Join it. This matters especially because employers now face a new uncertainty — "is this person's output their own ability, or AI's?" — and the only way to answer that question is to demonstrate your judgment visibly, consistently, in public.

That third point is actually the direction of my doctoral research. I'm trying to answer it from an organizational behavior perspective: how do individuals "use AI well" in a way that doesn't let AI "use them up"? What kinds of human-AI collaboration preserve efficiency while also preserving human judgment, creativity, and sense of meaning?

I'll keep sharing my thinking here — about how AI is changing how we work, learn, and create, and how we can find our place in that change.
