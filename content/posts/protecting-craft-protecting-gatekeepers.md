In Kanchipuram, India, silk weavers carry a tradition that stretches back five centuries. Their saris are heritage-certified. Globally coveted. Museum-exhibited. The craft is sacred.

The weavers earn below the poverty line.

This is the paradox that Matthew Grimes, a scholar who studies craft markets, dropped into a recent workshop on AI in qualitative research.

## The Authenticity Dialectic

Academic research, especially qualitative research, has its own version of this paradox. We sacralise certain things:

- **Apprenticeship** — you learn by sitting at the feet of a master, not from a textbook (and certainly not from ChatGPT).
- **Deep reading** — not skimming, not summarizing, but the slow, careful absorption of a text over weeks.
- **Tacit knowledge** — the craft of doing good research that "cannot be reduced to templates, recipes, or methods."
- **Elite certification** — your work is legitimate because *Administrative Science Quarterly* says so, or because your advisor's advisor was Karl Weick.

These aren't fake values. Deep reading matters. Mentorship matters. Tacit knowledge is real.

But here's the thing Grimes forces you to see: **every single one of these values is also a gatekeeping mechanism.**

Who gets apprenticed? Whoever the right advisor chooses. Who develops tacit knowledge? Whoever has access to the right seminars, the right conferences, the right networks. Who gets published in elite journals? Whoever can navigate a system that rewards connection as much as contribution.

Grimes argued: "When access to practice, feedback, and refining depends on gatekeepers, the sacralizing of craft becomes a mechanism of closure."

The very language we use to protect what's valuable about scholarship — *it can't be templated, it can't be automated, it requires years of immersion* — is also the language that keeps the gates locked. Not because anyone intends it that way. But because that's how systems of legitimacy work.

## Necessary But Not Sufficient

Grimes is not dismissing the concerns about AI in academia. He's explicitly responding to a 2025 paper by Beth Bechky and Gerald Davis in *Administrative Science Quarterly* — "Resisting the Algorithmic Management of Science" — and he gives them real credit:

- Yes, deep reading and intellectual engagement are genuinely at risk.
- Yes, the reforms they propose to protect apprenticeship are important.
- Yes, these are defenses of genuine values.

"But," Grimes adds, "they don't address who the current model excludes — or whether our outputs justify the process."

That's the line that cuts.

Because if we're honest, the "slow scholarship" model has produced a lot of work that doesn't matter much. The median citation count for a management journal article is... not inspiring. And the process that produces those articles is extraordinarily resource-intensive: years of PhD training, multiple rounds of revision, decades of career building.

If this process reliably produced transformative knowledge, the cost would be justified. But does it?

## The Uncomfortable Potential of AI

Grimes doesn't claim AI will save academia. He's careful: AI has "the potential — not the guarantee — to democratize our profession."

What would that look like?

- **Less dependence on a single advisor's network** for access to methods and craft knowledge. If AI can teach you grounded theory at 2 AM, you don't need to have won the advisor lottery.
- **More diverse pathways into the profession.** If craft knowledge can be partially externalized, the barriers to entry drop — for first-generation scholars, for researchers in the global south, for anyone outside the prestige pipeline.

But Grimes immediately flags the risks:

- **The seminar room, the conference, the co-authorship — this is where community lives.** If AI replaces the need for these spaces, we lose something that makes scholarship human.
- **If AI replaces the intellectual friction of human mentorship, we lose something irreplaceable.** A good advisor doesn't just teach you methods — they challenge your thinking in ways that hurt and that grow you. AI can answer your questions. It can't make you uncomfortable in the right way.

He calls this **asymmetric empowerment**: "We gain capability while losing comprehension. AI lets us do more while understanding less about how we're doing it."

## The Split

Grimes' conclusion is the most provocative. He argues the field is splitting — not into "pro-AI" and "anti-AI," but into two markets:

**The Craft Market** protects the sacredness of process. It values apprenticeship and slow scholarship. Its risk? Prestige enclosure — replicating the very hierarchies that exclude.

**The Discovery Market** embraces AI for discovery, rigor, and impact. It values outcomes and societal contribution. Its risk? Shallow engagement with ideas — breadth without depth.

Neither is wrong. Both have failure modes. And Grimes doesn't tell you which to choose. He just insists that you choose honestly.

And then he leaves you with this: "If we can produce knowledge faster, we have less excuse for producing knowledge that doesn't matter."

I haven't been able to stop thinking about that sentence.
