Choosing a research topic is the first thing we do when entering the research process. At conferences, it's also the most common question you get asked. Whether you're a PhD student in your early stage, in your late stage looking to pivot, or an early-career faculty member finding your footing, choosing a good research topic matters. But how?

In their *Publishing in AMJ* series, Colquitt and George proposed five criteria for effective topics: significance, novelty, curiosity, scope, and actionability. Here are the core insights from each.

---

## 1. Significance: Connecting to a Grand Challenge

Significance does not mean grandiosity. Not every study needs to introduce a new paradigm. Cumulativeness matters. The point is not that your study must change the world. It should articulate how solving this particular puzzle contributes to a larger one.

Colquitt and George frame this as confronting "grand challenges": bold ideas and less conventional approaches to tackling large, unresolved problems. Posing your topic within a grand challenge framework gives voice to your study's reason for existing.

A narrow mechanism study can be significant if it connects to a problem that matters. A broad phenomenon study can be insignificant if it never explains why anyone should care.

---

## 2. Novelty: Avoiding the Three Traps

Scientific work is a conversation among scholars. The simplest test for novelty: would this study change the conversation already taking place? Does it merely add to the momentum, or does it cause heads to turn?

Novel topics often come from knowledge recombination: building bridges between literatures that haven't spoken to each other.

But three traps make novelty hard:

- **The familiarity trap**: picking a topic you know too well, so the contribution feels like a marginal extension.
- **The maturity trap**: entering a literature that has already been thoroughly worked, so your addition feels redundant.
- **The nearness trap**: choosing something adjacent to existing work but not different enough. Close enough to seem safe. Too close to feel new.

All three traps reward you emotionally while punishing you professionally. Familiar topics are comfortable. Mature literatures have clear methods. Adjacent topics feel innovative without requiring genuine departure.

---

## 3. Curiosity: The Mystery Test

This is the criterion I find most useful. The best research topics work like mysteries. You should not be able to guess the ending from the title.

Consider: "The Effects of Leader Displays of Happiness on Team Performance." You already know where this is going. Leader is happy, team performs better. Why read eighty pages to confirm what you assumed in the first sentence?

Now consider Van Kleef and colleagues: does leader happiness *or anger* drive team performance, and is the mechanism emotional contagion or cold inferential reasoning? You genuinely cannot predict the answer. The topic earns your attention because it creates an information gap your mind wants to close.

Davis argued in 1971 that propositions are interesting precisely when they deny the audience's taken-for-granted assumptions. The Chinese have a perfect expression for this: something that is *reasonable yet unexpected*. If your topic only confirms what everyone already believes, it is not interesting. It is redundant.

How to arouse curiosity? Challenge existing assumptions. Use mystery as a metaphor. If the ending is clear and obvious from the title, the topic has failed.

---

## 4. Scope: Sampling the Full Landscape

Scope is about whether you adequately sample the landscape of relevant constructs, mechanisms, and perspectives. Studies cannot tackle grand challenges if they are not ambitious in scope.

The best topics fully sample the landscape, possibly using multiple lenses. Seibert, Kraimer, and Liden (2001), for example, examined social capital and career success using three theoretical perspectives simultaneously, operationalizing mediators for each.

Better to start ambitious and trim in revision than to begin too narrow.

---

## 5. Actionability: The Reality Check

Actionability functions as a reality check on the other four. You can have a novel, curiosity-inducing, significant topic with ambitious scope, and it can still fail if practitioners read the findings and shrug.

McGahan names five ways management research can be actionable: counterintuitive insights, new practices, inconsistencies in existing practices, theories that explain current situations, and iconic phenomena that open new inquiry. The common thread: the research speaks to variables within the control of managers.

Research has relevance when it generates insights that practitioners find useful for understanding their own organizational realities.

---

## Putting It Together

The five criteria are interconnected. The best topics represent grand challenges, are ambitious in scope, and offer novel, unconventional changes to existing conversations, while maintaining curiosity and practical relevance.

If I had to distill the editorial advice into one test:

**If someone reads your title and already knows the ending, your topic has failed before your study begins.**

The hardest part of research is not answering questions well. It is finding questions that deserve the effort.
