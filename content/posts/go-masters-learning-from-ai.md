In 2016, Google DeepMind's AlphaGo defeated Lee Sedol — one of the greatest Go players in history — in a match that shocked the world. It was supposed to be AI's crowning moment. And it was.

But the more interesting story is what happened *after*.

Today, nearly every professional Go player trains with KataGo, an open-source AI that plays at a superhuman level. They study its moves. They memorize its patterns. They replicate its choices in tournament play. Over a third of top players' moves now come directly from AI suggestions.

And here's the paradox: **they're copying moves they don't understand.**

## The Best Teacher Who Can't Teach

Kim Chae-young, a rising star in Korean Go, put it simply: "It seems like it's thinking in a higher dimension... it's less about rationally thinking through each move, but more about developing a gut feeling."

The world's best players aren't *learning* from AI in any traditional sense. They're developing intuition by osmosis — absorbing patterns without grasping principles. It's less like studying with a master and more like copying from an answer key you can't read.

Lee Sedol, the man AlphaGo defeated, saw this coming. He retired from professional Go in 2019, saying: "If you copy your moves from an answer key, that's no longer art."

Google DeepMind researchers managed to extract new chess concepts from AlphaZero and teach them to grandmasters. But doing the same for Go? Still an unsolved problem. The AI's knowledge is locked in a black box that no one — not even its creators — can fully open.

## The Science Behind the Surrender

What's happening in Go isn't just a sports story. Researchers at Wharton have now given it a name.

In a recent paper, Steven Shaw and Gideon Nave introduce what they call **Tri-System Theory** — an extension of the famous "thinking fast and slow" framework. You probably know Kahneman's two systems: System 1 (fast, intuitive) and System 2 (slow, deliberate). Shaw and Nave add a third: **System 3 — artificial cognition that operates outside the brain.**

System 3 is the AI you consult. The chatbot you ask. The model whose answer you accept.

The key prediction of their theory is what they call **"cognitive surrender"** — the tendency to adopt AI outputs with minimal scrutiny, effectively overriding both your gut instinct (System 1) and your careful reasoning (System 2).

And their experiments confirm it. Across three preregistered studies with over 1,300 participants and nearly 10,000 trials:

- **Participants chose to consult AI on the majority of trials** (>50%) — even when they could solve problems themselves
- **When AI was accurate, accuracy jumped +25 percentage points.** When AI was wrong, accuracy dropped 15 points. People followed the machine either way.
- **Most striking: AI increased people's confidence even when it gave them wrong answers.** They felt *more* certain while being *more* wrong.

Time pressure didn't fix it. Financial incentives didn't fix it. Feedback didn't fix it. The surrender pattern persisted across every condition.

Now reread what Kim Chae-young said about Go: "It's less about rationally thinking through each move, but more about developing a gut feeling."

That *is* cognitive surrender. System 3 has replaced System 2. The world's best players aren't deliberating — they're deferring.

## This Isn't Just About Go

If this sounds familiar, it should. The same dynamic is playing out in every office, studio, and classroom where people work alongside AI.

A marketing manager uses ChatGPT to write copy. The output is good — maybe better than what they'd write themselves. They ship it. But do they understand *why* it works? Could they replicate the logic without the tool? Shaw and Nave would say: System 3 has been consulted. System 2 has been bypassed. The manager feels confident. The surrender is complete.

A junior developer copies a code solution from Copilot. It passes the tests. But when something breaks three months later, they can't debug it — because they never understood the underlying architecture.

A student generates an essay outline with Claude. The structure is elegant. But ask them to explain the reasoning behind it, and they freeze.

**The pattern is the same everywhere**: AI gives us the *what* but not the *why*. And as Shaw and Nave's data shows, we don't just accept the what — we become *more confident* in it, even when it's wrong. That's the truly unsettling part. Cognitive surrender doesn't feel like giving up. It feels like knowing.

## What's Actually at Stake

This isn't an argument against using AI. The Go world shows us that too — AI *democratized* the game. Female players who were historically excluded from elite male training circles suddenly had access to world-class instruction. Choi Jeong became the first woman to reach a major international final. Kim Chae-young won the Korean Go League playoffs as the only female player.

AI leveled the playing field. That's unambiguously good.

But it also leveled the *thinking*. Opening moves, which used to be a canvas for personal philosophy and creative expression, are now standardized AI recommendations. The game got more equal — and more uniform.

Shaw and Nave's research adds a crucial nuance: the people most prone to cognitive surrender are those with **higher trust in AI, lower need for cognition, and lower fluid intelligence**. In other words, the people who most need to think carefully are the ones most likely to stop thinking. AI doesn't just replace deliberation — it removes the *felt need* to deliberate.

The real question isn't whether to use AI as a teacher. It's **how to learn from a teacher that can't explain itself — without surrendering the very thinking it was supposed to enhance.**

## Three Questions Worth Sitting With

1. **When you use AI output in your work, do you understand *why* it's good?** Not just that it sounds right — but the underlying logic? Or has System 3 quietly replaced your System 2?

2. **Are you building skill, or building dependence?** Shaw and Nave found that even financial incentives couldn't override cognitive surrender. If incentives can't snap you out of it, what can?

3. **What's your "opening move"?** In Go, that's where personality lives. In your work, what's the part that's uniquely, irreducibly *you* — and are you still investing in it?

Shin Jin-seo, the current world #1, offers a clue. He doesn't just copy AI moves — he actively tries to understand *why* the AI chose them. "I can play a kind of Go that tells a story that only a human can," he says.

In Shaw and Nave's language, Shin refuses to surrender. He uses System 3 as input for System 2 — not as a replacement for it. The AI proposes; he interrogates. That's the difference between using AI and being used by it.

The black box isn't going away. The question is whether you peer inside it — or let it think for you.
