Every PhD student using AI today is solving the same problem: how to produce more, faster. The output improves — literature reviews done in an hour, arguments structured before the coffee gets cold, drafts that no longer embarrass on first read. But this frames doctoral training as a *production* problem. It isn't only that.

A PhD is also a *development* problem: the skills that make someone a researcher (reading carefully, building arguments, tolerating uncertainty) are built *through* the difficulty of producing output badly at first. AI removes that difficulty without announcing it. The output keeps improving; the skills quietly stop forming. Lu et al.'s *The AI Scientist* sharpens this to a point: a system that autonomously generates ideas, runs experiments, and produces manuscripts that passed peer review at a top-tier machine learning conference — the entire research lifecycle, no human required.

If the output can be fully automated, the only remaining argument for the PhD is the development that happens inside the researcher. Dries Faems, a professor studying innovation and technology at WHU – Otto Beisheim School of Management, named what we are losing precisely: *"These are exactly the activities that AI can make easier to skip."* But his post stopped there. It named the danger without showing the exit. This post takes his diagnosis further: for each of the four skills he identifies, here is what AI use looks like when it is weakening you, and what it looks like when it is building you instead.

---

## Four Skills, Four Wrong Patterns

Faems names four core PhD competencies. Here is how default AI use undermines each — and what using AI *well* looks like instead.

### Reading carefully

**The wrong pattern**: Ask AI to summarize papers. Read the summaries. Stay current with the literature without ever deeply engaging with it.

This feels efficient. And it is — for producing output. But careful reading is not primarily about knowing what a paper says. It's about building the cognitive templates for recognizing good arguments, noticing missing controls, understanding why a particular theoretical framing was chosen over alternatives. That happens through friction with the text itself, not through a smooth summary.

**The right pattern**: Read the paper first. Write your own summary and critique. *Then* ask AI: *"Here's my reading of this paper. What did I miss or misinterpret?"* You get the benefit of AI's breadth as a calibration tool, but the encoding happens through your own effortful generation. This is the Generation Effect (Slamecka & Graf, 1978) in practice: information you produce yourself sticks far better than information you passively receive.

### Developing arguments

**The wrong pattern**: Ask AI to generate a theoretical framework. Use the logical structure AI proposes. Build your dissertation on an argument you never had to construct from scratch.

**The right pattern**: State your argument to AI — and ask it to attack it. Ask it to steelman the opposing view, identify your weakest assumptions, find the gaps in your reasoning chain. Defending your position against AI's pushback is genuine intellectual exercise. You're not asking AI to think for you; you're asking it to sharpen the thinking you're doing yourself.

### Writing clearly

**The wrong pattern**: Ask AI to rewrite your drafts. Accept the improved version. Graduate writing more polished output, never understanding why your original was unclear.

**The right pattern**: Ask AI to diagnose, not fix. *"What is unclear in this paragraph, and why?"* Then rewrite it yourself. Ask AI to explain the principle behind its diagnosis — so you internalize the rule, not just the correction. Or write a section, ask AI to write its own version of the same section, then study the *differences*. Learn from the gap between your draft and the AI's draft. The goal is to understand, not to outsource.

### Dealing with uncertainty and complexity

This is the deepest one. Faems argues that tolerance for ambiguity — the ability to sit with a messy, unresolved problem and navigate it rather than escape it — is perhaps the defining skill of a researcher. It's what distinguishes someone who can generate original knowledge from someone who can synthesize existing knowledge.

**The wrong pattern**: Use AI to get quick, confident answers. Never sit with the mess. Arrive at a resolution before you've inhabited the complexity.

**The right pattern**: When stuck, ask AI to *map* the competing views on a question rather than resolve it. Ask it to lay out what remains unresolved. *Ask it: "What would it mean for my argument if assumption X turns out to be wrong?"* These prompts push you *into* complexity rather than out of it. You practice navigating uncertainty, with AI as a guide to the terrain rather than a helicopter out of it.

## The Counter-Intuitive Implication

If you take Faems' argument seriously, something follows that feels wrong at first.

**Using AI *well* should make your PhD harder, not easier. The cognitive load should go *up*, not down.**

If you're using AI correctly, you're reading papers in full before asking AI to challenge your reading. You're writing arguments before asking AI to attack them. You're producing drafts before asking AI to diagnose them. You're sitting with uncertain problems before asking AI to map the complexity. In every case, AI is the second step — not the first — and AI is making you do *more* intellectual work, not less.

---

## A Question to Sit With

If your AI use has made your PhD *easier*, there is a harder question worth asking: easier in which sense?

Easier to produce output? That's real and probably fine.

Easier to avoid the activities that build you into a researcher? That's the thing Faems is worried about. And the uncomfortable truth is that those two kinds of easier often feel identical from the inside. Cognitive surrender doesn't feel like giving up. It feels like being productive.

The struggle Faems warns about losing isn't inefficiency. It's the actual mechanism by which you become someone who can generate original knowledge. AI used well puts more of that struggle on your plate, not less.
